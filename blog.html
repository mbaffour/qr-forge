Introducing QR Forge —
Where Tradition Meets Technology

A QR code generator that doesn't just make links scannable — it makes them meaningful. Built with Ghanaian Adinkra symbols, Kente cloth patterns, and cultural motifs from around the world.

QR codes are everywhere — restaurant menus, business cards, conference name tags, event posters. But almost every tool that generates them treats them the same way: a black-and-white square that links to something.

We thought: what if a QR code could carry cultural identity as well as information? What if scanning a code at a Ghanaian entrepreneur's booth opened their website with a corner pattern drawn from the Adinkra symbol for wisdom? What if a Buddhist retreat's WiFi QR used an endless knot? What if a Māori artist's contact card had a koru spiral in every corner?

That's what QR Forge is built for.

What Makes It Different

🎨

🌍

🌓

Switch between three UI modes to suit your environment and preference. State is saved locally so you come back exactly where you left off.

The Symbols

Every QR code has three finder patterns — the square markers in three corners that help scanners orient the code. In QR Forge, these aren't just squares. They're drawn as culturally meaningful symbols from traditions around the world.

🇬🇭 Adinkra Symbols — Ghana

Adinkra symbols originate with the Akan people of Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire. Each symbol encodes a concept, a proverb, or a value — stamped onto cloth for ceremonies and worn as visual philosophy.

Open Source, Always Free

QR Forge is a single HTML file. No framework, no build step, no backend. Fork it, self-host it, modify it, embed it. The entire codebase — QR math, canvas renderer, cultural symbol drawing, UI — is in one file you can inspect and own.
